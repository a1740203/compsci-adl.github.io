Make A Game

Programming Competition

Overview

Make A Game is a programming competition, where teams get together to design and develop their own game, and be in the chance to win some sweet prizes. It is a chance to finally fulfil that itch to work on your own project, and create something you want to do.

This is quite an open ended competition, but there will be an overarching theme that should be taken into consideration. You do not need to hand in any design documents. Your code will not be scrutinised for marks. In the end, it's the final product in all its glory (and bugs) that will be looked at.

Judging Each entry will be judged by a small panel of judges. Each submissions should include a short video that demonstrates all the features of your game. This will be the main point of judging for all minor awards and the People's Choice Award. Any major awards, will be run, played and tested. Source code should be provided for all submissions, but commenting/style/format will not be considered.
Criteria The submissions will be based around the following criteria:
  • Design concept
  • Core mechanics
  • Aesthetics
  • Theme involvement
Prizes Major Prizes
  • Golden Egg. The best game out of all the teams.
  • Golden Egg: Duckling. The best game from a Duckling team.
  • People's Choice. Publicly voted best game out of all games.

Rules

Language and Libraries
  • Can use any free programming language (eg: C++, Processing, Javascript).
  • Can use any library or framework (must be included in source).
  • Can NOT use pre-existing engine (eg: Unity, GameMaker, Unreal Engine), unless it is your own. We want to see you code, not drag and drop!
  • Strive to be able to run on University systems, both performance wise and technical wise.
Teams and Participants
  • Teams can be of up to 3 people.
  • Participants do NOT have to be a computer science students.
  • Participants must be current students at time of competition start.
  • Participants do not have to be from the same year level.
  • A team can only qualify as a "Duckling Team", if all of the participants are in their first year of computer science (or are not computer science students).
  • The majority of the team must be from the University of Adelaide. Any other participants still must be students.
Submission
  • Source code must be available for review and judging.
  • An accompanying video, demonstrating your game and all its major features is required. Upload the video to some streaming service (eg: YouTube.com, Vimeo.com), and then provide the link.
  • A text README is also required, that includes the following:
    • Instructions on how to build and run the game.
    • Dependencies required to build the game.
    • Game controls and instructions.
    • Team name and team members.
  • Submission of code is done by any of the following (most preferred to least preferred method):
    • Web link submission (eg: github.com)
    • Email to competition@csclub.org.au
    • Physical hand-in to committee member (Sam)
Your Work, Assets and Licensing

We want to see YOUR work! You may have a great idea to recreate Asteroids, or have your own Overwatch, but we want to encourage you away from that. We want to see the coolest, most innovative design you and your team can create. Even if your game isn't finished in the end, it's the overall concept that has the most impact.

As there are real prizes you can win, and we want you to showcase your work, you will need to take care with the assets that you use. Any images, music, libraries or similar that you may use, must be free for use, for commercial or non-commercial use. If in doubt, look at the licensing or email the link/asset with its license to competition@csclub.org.au. You can of course make your own assets if you want!

Any submission you make, still belongs to you. We are NOT going to steal it and claim it. However, if we really like your submission, we may ask you if we can use your submission or video in future showcases.
